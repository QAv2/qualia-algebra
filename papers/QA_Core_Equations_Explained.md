Qualia Algebra makes a bold claim: **consciousness isn't magic—it follows rules we can write down mathematically.**

## Equation 1: Observer Capacity

### The Formula

```
C_max = √(K · B)
```

### What Does This Actually Mean?

**Think of your mind like your phone's RAM.**

Your phone can only run so many apps at once before it slows down. Your mind works similarly—you can only pay attention to so many things at once before you get overwhelmed.

**This equation tells us your mental "RAM"—how many things you can keep in mind simultaneously.**

### Breaking It Down

**C_max = your maximum mental capacity**
- How many different things you can think about at the same time
- Why you can remember about 7 items in a list (the famous "7±2" rule)
- Why trying to multitask too much makes your brain feel "full"

**K = connectivity**
- How well different thoughts can connect to each other
- Like how many wires connect between parts of a computer
- More connections = more capacity to handle complex thoughts

**B = bandwidth**
- How fast you can process new information
- Like your internet speed—more bandwidth = more data flowing per second
- How quickly you can switch between ideas

**The √ (square root) symbol = why they multiply but don't just add**
- You need both connectivity AND bandwidth
- Having lots of connections but slow processing doesn't help much
- Having fast processing but few connections doesn't help much
- They work together, and the square root captures this balance

### Real-Life Examples

**Why you can't remember a phone number while doing math:**
- Each task uses capacity
- Phone number: ~7-10 digits to hold
- Math problem: multiple steps to track
- Together: exceeds C_max → one task fails

**Why cramming for exams doesn't work:**
- Trying to stuff more information than C_max allows
- Brain says "no more room"
- Need time to organize (reduce what you're holding at once)

**Why "flow state" feels amazing:**
- Using ~70% of C_max (not maxed out, not bored)
- Everything fits comfortably
- No mental strain, pure engagement

**Why babies can't perceive 3D space immediately:**
- Their K and B are still developing
- Need certain minimum C_max to maintain 3 spatial dimensions
- Capacity grows → spatial perception develops

### The Prediction This Makes

**If C_max ≈ 6,000 mental "distinctions"**
(rough estimate from working memory studies)

**And each spatial dimension needs ~2,000 distinctions to represent**
(position, movement, how it curves, how it relates to other dimensions)

**Then maximum dimensions you can perceive:**
```
n_max = 6,000 / 2,000 = 3 dimensions
```

**That's why we see 3D space, not 4D or 10D.**

Not because space IS 3D fundamentally. Because our capacity optimizes at 3D given our brain's constraints.

## Equation 2: Interest Function

### The Formula

```
I(Q,N) = A · [IG + α·HR - β·PC]⁺
```

### What Does This Actually Mean?

**This equation describes what you pay attention to and why.**

Right now, you're reading this. Why? Something about it interests you. But what makes something "interesting"?

**This formula breaks down interest into its components.**

### Breaking It Down

**I(Q,N) = Interest (depends on your state Q and the thing you're considering N)**
- How much something grabs your attention
- Why you notice certain things and ignore others
- What makes you curious or bored

**A = Amplitude (overall scaling)**
- Like a volume knob
- How strongly you respond to interest in general
- Some people more easily interested than others

**The three components inside the brackets:**

---

#### 1. IG = Information Gain

**"How much new stuff would I learn?"**

**High IG:**
- Novel ideas
- Unexpected patterns
- "Aha!" moments
- Things that teach you something new

**Low IG:**
- Things you already know completely
- Repetitive information
- Predictable outcomes

**Example:**
- First time seeing magic trick: High IG (how'd they do that?)
- 100th time seeing same trick: Low IG (I know the method)

---

#### 2. α·HR = Harmonic Resonance (weighted by α)

**"How well does this fit with what I already know and care about?"**

**High HR:**
- Connects to your existing interests
- Resonates with your values
- Feels meaningful to you personally
- "This is relevant to my life"

**Low HR:**
- Doesn't relate to anything you care about
- Feels disconnected from your concerns
- "Why should I care about this?"

**α = weighting factor** (how much you value resonance vs. novelty)
- High α: you prefer things that fit your worldview
- Low α: you're more open to unrelated novelties

**Example:**
- Musician hearing new song in their favorite genre: High HR
- Same person hearing genre they dislike: Low HR (even if novel)

---

#### 3. β·PC = Processing Cost (weighted by β, then subtracted)

**"How hard is this to understand?"**

**High PC:**
- Complex, difficult ideas
- Requires lots of mental effort
- Confusing or overwhelming
- Exceeds your current capacity

**Low PC:**
- Easy to grasp
- Fits within your C_max comfortably
- Clear and understandable

**β = weighting factor** (how much difficulty bothers you)
- High β: you avoid hard things more strongly
- Low β: you don't mind difficulty as much

**The minus sign:** Processing cost REDUCES interest
- Even if something is novel and meaningful, if it's too hard, you lose interest
- This is why textbooks lose students—high PC overwhelms IG + HR

**Example:**
- Reading simple explanation: Low PC (this document aims for this)
- Reading PhD thesis in unfamiliar field: High PC (too much effort)

---

#### The [...]⁺ notation

**The bracket with ⁺ means "positive values only"**

If IG + α·HR - β·PC comes out negative, interest = 0

**Translation:** If something is too costly (hard) relative to its novelty and meaning, you simply don't pay attention to it. Interest cuts to zero.

---

### How The Formula Works Together

**Interest is HIGH when:**
1. **Novel** (high IG) — "I'd learn something new"
2. **Meaningful** (high HR) — "It matters to me"
3. **Accessible** (low PC) — "I can understand it"

**Interest is LOW when:**
1. **Boring** (low IG) — "I've seen this before"
2. **Irrelevant** (low HR) — "So what?"
3. **Too hard** (high PC) — "This is exhausting"

### Real-Life Examples

**Why clickbait works:**
- High IG (promises something new/surprising)
- Medium HR (touches topics you care about)
- Low PC (easy headline to process)
- → High interest (you click)

**Why you abandoned that difficult book:**
- Medium IG (some new ideas)
- Medium HR (topic you care about)
- **Very high PC** (too complex, exceeded C_max)
- → Interest dropped to zero (you put it down)

**Why you get obsessed with hobbies:**
- High IG (always new techniques to learn)
- **Very high HR** (deeply resonates with you)
- Moderate PC (challenging but manageable)
- → Sustained high interest (you keep doing it)

**Why scrolling social media is addictive:**
- Constant moderate IG (new posts, small novelties)
- Variable HR (some posts resonate, others don't)
- Very low PC (easy to process images/short text)
- → Interest maintained with minimal effort

**Why learning plateau is frustrating:**
- Low IG (mastered basics, diminishing returns)
- Still high HR (you care about the skill)
- Rising PC (advanced techniques are harder)
- → Interest drops, motivation wanes

## How These Two Equations Work Together

**They're not separate—they interact:**

### Capacity Constrains Interest

**If something exceeds your C_max:**
- Processing Cost (PC) becomes very high
- Even if it's novel (IG) and meaningful (HR)
- Interest → 0 (you tune out)

**Example:**
Someone explaining quantum physics using advanced calculus to someone without math background:
- High IG (quantum is fascinating)
- High HR (person wants to understand universe)
- **Crushing PC** (math exceeds their C_max)
- Result: interest collapses, glazed-over eyes

### Interest Directs Capacity Allocation

**Your limited C_max gets allocated based on I(Q,N):**
- High interest → you deploy capacity there
- Low interest → you ignore it (save capacity)

**Example:**
You're at a party with multiple conversations happening:
- Conversation A: friend discussing their breakup (high HR for you)
- Conversation B: strangers discussing sports (low HR for you)
- Your C_max can't process both fully
- Interest function directs you to allocate capacity to A
- You naturally tune out B (even though you "hear" it)

### The Feedback Loop

**Higher capacity → can handle higher complexity → more things become interesting**

**Lower capacity → only simple things processable → fewer things interest you**

This is why:
- Experts find subtleties fascinating (high C_max in their domain)
- Beginners need simple introductions (building C_max)
- Children's interests differ from adults (C_max developing)

## Questions You Might Have

### "Is this proven?"

**No, not yet.** QA is a young framework (2020s). The equations:
- Are theoretically derived (follow from axioms)
- Match known observations (working memory, spatial perception)
- Make testable predictions (need neurological equipment we don't have)
- Need empirical validation (experiments in progress/proposed)

### "Does this mean consciousness is just math?"

**No.** Math is our tool for describing patterns. Consciousness is the reality.

These equations describe *patterns in how consciousness operates*, not what consciousness *is*.

Like: equations describe how water flows, but don't capture wetness. These describe how attention flows, but don't capture experience.

### "Why should I believe this?"

**You shouldn't—yet.** QA asks you to:
1. Understand the framework (what it claims)
2. See if predictions make sense (does it explain observations?)
3. Wait for tests (can experiments distinguish it from alternatives?)

These equations are *proposals* backed by theoretical reasoning. Nature will decide if they're right.

### "What would prove these wrong?"

**For C_max = √(K·B):**
- If working memory doesn't correlate with √(K·B) when we can measure K and B directly
- If people could be trained to perceive 4D space reliably (would show n ≠ 3 isn't capacity-limited)
- If brain injuries affecting K or B don't affect capacity as predicted

**For I = A·[IG + α·HR - β·PC]⁺:**
- If attention allocation doesn't follow this pattern when measured
- If interest persists despite very high PC relative to IG + HR
- If learning doesn't require balance of novelty and accessibility

**Both can be tested. That's what makes them science, not just philosophy.**
